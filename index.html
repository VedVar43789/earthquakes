The Ring of Fire

Every 72 hours, somewhere along this glowing ring that encircles our planet, the Earth fractures with a violence that defies comprehension.

The Pacific Ring of Fire stretches 40,000 kilometers around the world's largest ocean—a horseshoe of tectonic fury that begins in New Zealand, arcs through Japan and Alaska, and plunges down the western coasts of North and South America before closing the circle in Chile.

200 Million Years in Motion

Along this boundary, massive plates of the Earth's crust grind against each other in slow-motion collisions that have been reshaping our planet for 200 million years.

Cities on the Edge

Here's what makes this ring of geological violence truly remarkable: it's also where humanity has chosen to build its greatest cities.

Tokyo sits directly above three converging tectonic plates. San Francisco straddles the San Andreas Fault. Los Angeles sprawls across a web of active fault lines. Jakarta, Manila, Lima, Santiago—each a megacity of millions, each perched on the very seams where our planet comes apart.

Dangerous Paradise

This isn't coincidence. The same geological forces that create devastating earthquakes also create the conditions for human civilization to flourish.

Volcanic soils are among the world's most fertile. Tectonic activity creates natural harbors and mineral wealth. Mountain ranges forged by colliding plates provide fresh water and defensive barriers.

For 10,000 years, we've been drawn to these dangerous edges because they're also the most valuable real estate on Earth.

2.5 Billion Lives at Risk

Today, 2.5 billion people—nearly one in three humans—live within 100 kilometers of a major fault line along the Ring of Fire.
